About Us

The MIT Lion Dance Team is a troupe based at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

The group has been performing since 1994, and was recognized as an official club at MIT in 1997.

As a club, we hope to both introduce the traditional art form of lion dance and spread fortune to as many people as possible throughout the Greater Boston Area!

About Lion Dance

Introduction

Lion dance is one of China's most distinctive cultural arts. This form of traditional dance dates is believed to have appeared in either the Tang, Han, or Qin dynasties. Some legends associated with lion dance include using the dance to chase away the "Nian" monster.

Often performed by Kung Fu schools, lion dancing brings good fortune, prosperity, and longevity. It is usually performed during cultural celebrations such as during Lunar New Year, as well as to bless the opening of new shops, weddings, or births.

Costume

Lion dance costumes often don't look like the lions we're familiar with. Lions were not common in Ancient China, resulting in the differences in appearance. The costume was designed to have bright colors to chase away evil spirits and bad luck. The horn is used to ward off bad spirits, but beware -- touching the horn will bring you bad luck!

There are different styles of lions, with the Northern and Southern being the main types in China. The Northern lion looks more realistic, with fur all over the body and a more anatomically correct head. The performers often wear leggings that add to the realism. The Southern lion head is more ornate and fancy. The body and tail is made of silk and has almost no fur.

Dance

There are several elements that can be found in a typical lion dance performance. Often, the introduction will include a bow to show respect, and the remainder may include fighting (if more than one lion is performing), sleeping, grooming, eating, and drinking. Many dances will also add in tricks such as lifts and rolls.

Generally, each dance contains a sequence, called Choy Cheng, where the lion eats lettuce or other greens. In the United States, green has come to mean prosperity as it is the color of money. Traditionally, however, this sequence is done because dealing out lettuce in Mandarin Chinese ("fa tsai") sounds similar to the phrase for “become prosperous”. Often a red envelope, called Hong Bao, is attached as payment. After eating the lettuce, the lion crouches for a moment and then sprays the audience with it. Sometimes the lettuce is placed behind obstacles that the lion needs to pass to get it.

Other exotic objects have been eaten with different meanings. Sometimes, scrolls with lucky sayings are used when spraying lettuce is unsuitable.

Other choreographed sequences may include a dancing Buddhist monk, called the Little Buddha, that provokes the lion, plays with it, and feeds it. Sometimes the lion will even bite the monk.

Legend and Origin

Despite being a large part of Chinese culture today, lions are not native to Ancient China and were likely introduced from other Asian countries. In fact, lion dance was described in the Tang Dynasty as a "foreign dance". Even then, there are several legends about the origin of lion dancing. One of the more popular legends details the chasing away of the "Nian" monster.

Nian was a fierce monster that especially liked kidnapping children. He terrorized villages annually right as one year turned into the next. Finally, one year, a lion defeated and chased the monster away, but Nian vowed to return the next year. This time, unfortunately, the villagers did not have a lion to protect themselves. They solved the problem by creating a costume in the likeness of a lion and two villagers used it to scare Nian away. Legend also says that loud noises from the drum, other instruments, and firecrackers helped to scare Nian away. Red is worn during New Years celebrations because it was also believed that Nian was afraid of the color red.

The Little Buddha originates from the Buddhist/Shaolin monks that were thought to have trained the first lion dancers. The Little Buddha is sometimes referred to as the Dai Tou Fut, or big headed monk.
